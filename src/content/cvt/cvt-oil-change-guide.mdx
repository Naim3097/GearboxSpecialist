Open a Malaysian owner's manual and you'll likely find the CVT fluid listed as "inspect" — or worse, "lifetime fill". Ask the engineers who build these transmissions and you get a different answer. The gap between those two answers is where most CVT failures in this country come from.

## What "lifetime" actually means

"Lifetime fluid" means the fluid is designed to last the *warranted* life of the vehicle under *reference* conditions — moderate climates, flowing traffic. It's a maintenance-cost marketing figure, not an engineering one.

Malaysian conditions break both assumptions. Ambient heat starts the fluid closer to its thermal limit; stop-go traffic keeps it there for hours at a time. Fluid analysis on Klang Valley cars routinely shows 100,000 km "lifetime" fluid with the friction package depleted and oxidation well advanced.

In a CVT this matters more than in any other gearbox, because the fluid isn't just a lubricant — it's the thing that stops a steel belt slipping against steel pulleys. Clamping pressure and friction coefficient are calibrated around fresh fluid. Old fluid means micro-slip; micro-slip means metal dust; metal dust means the valve body, bearings and pulley faces all wear faster. It's a compounding loop.

## The intervals that actually protect the gearbox

| Driving pattern | Interval |
| --- | --- |
| Mostly city / daily jams (KL, PJ commuting) | 40,000 km |
| Mixed | 50,000 km |
| Mostly highway | 60,000 km |
| E-hailing / commercial | 30,000 km |

If the car's history is unknown — you bought it used, no receipts — treat the fluid as overdue and have its condition inspected before deciding anything.

## The specification rule

Every CVT is calibrated around one fluid chemistry:

- **Toyota:** CVT Fluid TC / FE
- **Honda:** HCF-2
- **Nissan / Mitsubishi (JATCO):** NS-2 / NS-3 per model
- **Perodua D-CVT:** Daihatsu AMMIX CVT spec
- **Proton Punch:** the Punch-specified fluid, not generic

The brand on the bottle matters far less than the specification on it. "Multi-vehicle CVT fluid" is formulated to be *acceptable* in many gearboxes and *ideal* in none — and judder complaints after a cheap service with universal fluid are a weekly sight at specialist workshops.

## What a proper CVT service includes

A real CVT service is more than a drain plug:

1. **Fluid condition assessment** — colour, smell, debris on the drain plug magnet. This is diagnosis, free.
2. **Filter replacement** where the unit has a serviceable filter (many do; quick services skip it).
3. **Correct-spec fluid**, filled to the level-check procedure — CVTs are sensitive to over- and under-fill.
4. **Adaptation reset / relearn** so the control unit recalibrates its pressure maps to the new fluid.
5. **A scan of internal counters** — fluid degradation counters and temperature history on many models tell you exactly how the gearbox has lived.

Skipping steps 4 and 5 is how a RM 250 service turns into "the fluid change made it worse".

## The economics, plainly

A CVT fluid service costs RM 250–400. A CVT overhaul costs RM 4,000–7,500. On the intervals above, you'd need to buy **ten to fifteen services** before you'd spent overhaul money — spread over roughly half a million kilometres. There is no cheaper insurance in motoring.
