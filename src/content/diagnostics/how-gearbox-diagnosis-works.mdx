Two cars arrive at two workshops with the same symptom: a jerk on the 2–3 shift. The first workshop says "gearbox spoilt, overhaul, RM 5,000." The second spends forty minutes measuring and finds a solenoid with a worn seal — RM 900. Same symptom, RM 4,100 apart. The difference wasn't luck; it was diagnosis.

## Why guessing is the most expensive option

A gearbox is a sealed hydraulic computer. From outside, a dozen different faults produce near-identical symptoms: a slip can be fluid, pressure, solenoid, valve body or clutch; a judder can be converter, mounts, or a misfiring engine. Parts-swapping through that list at RM 800–3,000 a guess is how a small fault becomes a big invoice **with the original problem still there.**

Diagnosis is the process of making the gearbox tell you which one it is — before any parts money moves.

## The five layers of a real diagnosis

### 1. The interview

When did it start? Hot or cold? Which gear, what speed, under load or lifting off? Service history? A precise symptom description eliminates half the possibilities before the bonnet opens.

### 2. The road test

A specialist drives the car through a deliberate protocol — every gear, hot and cold, light and full throttle, manual selection — with a scanner logging live data the whole time. The goal is to reproduce the symptom while the data is watching.

### 3. The scan — the real one

The transmission module's own data, not a generic engine scan:

- **Fault codes with freeze-frames** — what the gearbox saw at the moment of fault
- **Slip counters** per clutch or the CVT belt
- **Fluid temperature history** — has this unit been overheated, and how often?
- **Adaptation values** — how hard the computer is already compensating for wear; often the single most predictive number in the file
- **Live pressures and solenoid currents** against specification

### 4. Fluid evidence

Colour, smell, and what's on the magnet. Fine grey paste is normal wear; glitter is bearing or hard-part damage; friction material means clutches. Thirty seconds of looking, and it can overrule everything else.

### 5. Physical checks

Mounts, driveshafts, wiring, connectors, leaks. A worn engine mount mimics a gearbox jerk perfectly, and a RM 200 mount has embarrassed many RM 5,000 quotes.

## The output you should receive

A diagnosis is a document, not a vibe: the confirmed fault, the evidence behind it, a firm quote for the fix, and — honestly stated — anything that can't be confirmed without teardown, quoted as its own decision. If a workshop can't tell you *what* is wrong and *how they know*, they're asking you to fund an experiment.

## The economics

Diagnosis at a specialist costs RM 100–300. The average saving, against parts-swapping or an unnecessary overhaul, is measured in thousands — and the repair that follows actually fixes the car, first time. It's not a fee before the repair. It's the part of the repair that decides whether the rest of it is money well spent.
