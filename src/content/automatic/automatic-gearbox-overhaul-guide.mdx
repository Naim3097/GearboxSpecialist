"Overhaul" is the most abused word in Malaysian gearbox repair. It can mean a full engineering rebuild to factory tolerances — or a wash, new seals, and a prayer. Both get invoiced as overhauls. This guide is how you tell them apart.

## What a real overhaul involves

### 1. Teardown and root-cause inspection

The gearbox comes out and comes fully apart — every clutch drum opened, every bearing off. The point isn't just to replace what's burnt; it's to find **why** it burnt. A cooked clutch pack usually has an upstream cause: a worn valve-body bore dropping pressure, a leaking sealing ring, a blocked cooler. Replace the clutches without fixing the cause and the "overhaul" lasts a year.

### 2. Measurement against specification

Bores, end-float, clearances checked against the factory manual. Hard parts — planetary gears, drums, shafts, pump — are measured, not eyeballed. This is the step cosmetic rebuilds skip entirely.

### 3. The parts that must be new

- **Every friction plate and steel plate** — not just the burnt ones
- **Full soft-part kit:** every seal, O-ring, gasket and sealing ring
- **Bushings and thrust washers**
- **Filter**, and the **band** where fitted
- **Torque converter** rebuilt or replaced — reusing a debris-filled converter into a fresh gearbox is the classic comeback failure
- **Known-weakness updates:** improved friction materials, updated valve-body components, revised pistons where the model has documented issues

### 4. Valve body and electronics

The valve body is rebuilt or exchanged, solenoids tested to spec (not just "clicking"), and on mechatronic units the control module is bench-checked. The cooler circuit is flushed or replaced — old debris in the cooler is the other classic comeback.

### 5. Assembly, adaptation, road test

Clean-room assembly with measured clutch clearances, correct fluid, then adaptation relearn and a proper road test through every shift, hot and cold.

## What it costs in Malaysia

| Gearbox type | Typical overhaul cost |
| --- | --- |
| Perodua / Proton 4AT | RM 2,500 – 3,500 |
| Japanese 4–6AT (Toyota, Honda, Mazda) | RM 3,500 – 5,500 |
| CVT (Honda, Nissan, Proton) | RM 4,000 – 7,500 |
| DSG / DCT (VW, Proton 7DCT, Ford) | RM 5,000 – 9,000 |
| Continental AT (ZF, Mercedes 7G/9G) | RM 7,000 – 12,000+ |

Numbers vary with parts pricing and what the teardown finds — which is why an honest workshop quotes a range before teardown and a firm number after.

## Red flags that you're buying the cosmetic version

- A firm, suspiciously low price **before anyone has opened the gearbox**
- No teardown report or photos of your actual unit
- "Overhaul" completed in a day
- The old torque converter going straight back in
- No warranty, or a warranty with no mileage figure in writing
- The quote lists "gasket set" as the only parts

## The warranty test

One question filters most of the market: **"What's the warranty, in writing, in months and kilometres?"** A workshop confident in its rebuild offers 6–12 months. A workshop that plans to blame your driving offers excuses. Ask before you hand over the keys.
