Malaysia is close to a worst-case environment for automatic gearboxes: year-round heat, the region's heaviest urban traffic, sudden hill climbs, and a service culture trained to say "lifetime fluid". The maintenance schedule that follows isn't the one in your owner's manual — it's the one that matches how gearboxes actually age here.

## The master schedule

| Gearbox type | Fluid interval (MY conditions) | Also service |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Conventional automatic (4–8AT) | 60,000 km | Filter every 2nd fluid change; pan gasket as needed |
| CVT | 40,000–50,000 km | Filter where fitted; adaptation reset with every change |
| Wet dual-clutch (DSG DQ250/DQ381, Proton 7DCT) | 60,000 km — strict | Filter with every change |
| Dry dual-clutch (DQ200, Ford PowerShift) | Gear oil check 60,000 km | Clutch health scan yearly after 80,000 km |
| Hybrid e-CVT (Toyota/Honda/Lexus) | 80,000 km | Cooling system on schedule — heat is its only enemy |

Halve the interval for e-hailing, deliveries, frequent towing, or a daily commute that's mostly jams.

## Why Malaysian kilometres age fluid faster

Fluid life is a function of temperature, and temperature is a function of airflow and load. A gearbox cruising at 110 km/h runs cool. The same gearbox crawling the LDP at 14:00 — torque converter slipping, no airflow, aircon load on the engine — runs 20–30°C hotter, and every sustained 10°C above design temperature roughly halves fluid life. A KL car's 50,000 "traffic kilometres" load the fluid like a European car's 100,000.

That's the entire argument in one paragraph. The manual's schedule was written for the second car.

## The habits that matter more than the schedule

**Warm it before you work it.** Cold fluid is thick and clutch tolerances are off. The first two minutes of driving — not idling — should be gentle. Hard launches on a cold gearbox are the fastest wear you can buy.

**Stop fully before changing direction.** D to R while still rolling makes the clutches do the brakes' job. Once is nothing; a habit is a rebuild.

**On long climbs, use manual mode.** Genting-style ascents in D make some gearboxes hunt between ratios, which builds heat fast. Locking a gear steadies temperature.

**Don't ignore the first symptom.** Every gearbox failure story includes months of "it's been doing that a while". The first judder, flare or delay is the cheap moment.

## The pre-purchase rule

Buying used? The gearbox is the single most expensive unknown in the car. Ask for fluid receipts; if there are none, price in an immediate service **and** a specialist health scan — on DSG and CVT cars especially, a RM 300 inspection routinely saves buyers from RM 6,000 mistakes.

## What "servicing the gearbox" should mean

A proper service is fluid **to specification**, filter where serviceable, level set by the temperature procedure, adaptation reset, and a scan of temperature history and wear counters — with the old fluid's condition reported to you. If the invoice just says "ATF top up", the gearbox hasn't been serviced; it's been watered.
