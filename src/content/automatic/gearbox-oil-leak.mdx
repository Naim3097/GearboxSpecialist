A gearbox leak is the one fault that upgrades itself. Fluid loss lowers pressure; low pressure makes clutches slip; slipping makes heat; heat kills the fluid that's left, then the clutches. A RM 300 seal, ignored for a few months, buys a RM 5,000 overhaul. Reading the leak early is everything.

## Reading the patch under the car

Park over clean cardboard overnight:

- **Red / pink, thin** — automatic transmission fluid, fresh. The leak is active but the fluid is still healthy. Good news, act now.
- **Dark reddish-brown** — ATF that's aged or overheating. The leak has been there a while.
- **Brown-black with a burnt smell** — the fluid is cooked; assume internal damage is already beginning. This is a this-week problem.
- **Amber to black, thicker** — likely engine oil, not gearbox. Different problem, same advice.

Position matters: patches under the middle-rear of the engine bay (front-wheel drive) or along the transmission tunnel (rear-wheel drive) point at the gearbox.

## Where automatics actually leak

**The pan gasket and drain plug.** The commonest and cheapest. Heat cycles harden the gasket; on many German cars the pan is plastic and warps with age — ZF specifies pan replacement with every fluid service for exactly this reason.

**Axle / driveshaft seals.** Where the driveshafts enter the transaxle. Slow, messy leaks that fling fluid around the inner wheel area.

**The mechatronic sleeve (ZF 6HP/8HP).** The electrical connector's sealing sleeve hardens and weeps — the signature leak on BMWs. Cheap part, moderate labour, very worth doing with a fluid service.

**Cooler lines and unions.** Automatics pump fluid to a cooler; the unions and rubber sections age. Easy to spot, easy to fix, dangerous to ignore since a burst line dumps fluid in minutes.

**The front pump seal.** Between engine and gearbox — fluid appears at the bellhousing joint. The seal costs little; reaching it means removing the gearbox, so it's the expensive one, and often bundled with converter and clutch work while everything's out.

**Electrical connector seals.** Fluid wicks up the loom through a worn connector seal — the infamous "fluid in the plug" on Mercedes 722.6/722.9, which can travel up the harness into the control module.

## The urgency rule

Two questions set the timeline:

1. **How fast?** A damp film: book a repair this month. Drops overnight: this week. A puddle: don't drive it.
2. **How does it shift?** The moment shifting changes — flare, delay, harshness — the level has dropped far enough to affect pressure. From here, damage accumulates by the trip, not by the month.

## Why "just top it up" fails on modern cars

Most automatics since the mid-2000s have no dipstick. Level checks are done at a precise fluid temperature through a level plug, often needing a scan tool and a lift — deliberately workshop-only. Overfilling causes foaming and erratic shifting; underfilling burns clutches. Combined with fluid-specification sensitivity, DIY top-ups on sealed boxes cause almost as many problems as the leaks themselves.

Find the leak, fix the seal, service the fluid, done — while it's still a small job.
