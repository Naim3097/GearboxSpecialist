A CVT never fails without warning. Weeks or months before it stops driving, it tells you — through judder, noise, hesitation, or a warning light. The drivers who catch those signals early pay for a repair. The ones who don't pay for an overhaul, or a replacement gearbox.

This guide covers every meaningful CVT symptom, what each one means mechanically, and how urgent it really is.

## How a CVT actually works — in one paragraph

A CVT replaces fixed gears with a steel belt (or chain) running between two variable-width pulleys. Squeeze one pulley and open the other, and the ratio changes continuously — that's the whole trick. The belt is clamped by hydraulic pressure, which is why **fluid condition decides the life of the gearbox**: old fluid means lower clamping force, which means microscopic belt slip, which means metal dust circulating everywhere.

## Symptom by symptom

### Judder or shudder when moving off

The most common CVT complaint in Malaysia, and the signature issue on Honda Earth Dreams and Proton Punch units. The launch element — a torque converter on Hondas and Toyotas, a start clutch on Proton's Punch — grips unevenly as its friction surfaces or fluid degrade.

**Urgency: moderate.** Fresh, correct-specification fluid resolves early cases. Persistent judder means the converter or clutch needs work.

### Whining or droning that rises with road speed

Belt, pulley or bearing wear. A healthy CVT whines faintly — that's normal. A whine that gets louder over weeks, changes with speed rather than engine RPM, or turns into a growl is hardware wearing.

**Urgency: high.** Bearing debris destroys pulley surfaces. Caught early this is a repair; late, it's a rebuild.

### RPM flares but the car doesn't accelerate

Belt slip — the CVT equivalent of a slipping clutch. Clamping pressure can no longer hold the belt under load. Common on high-mileage JATCO (Nissan, Mitsubishi) units.

**Urgency: stop driving.** Every flare polishes metal off the belt and pulleys. This is the symptom that separates a four-figure repair from a five-figure one.

### Hesitation or delay when shifting from P to D

Line pressure is slow to build: degraded fluid, a tired pump, or a sticking valve body. On cold mornings a one-second delay can be normal; three seconds, or a clunk on engagement, is not.

**Urgency: moderate** — and cheap to fix now.

### Limp mode after hard or hot driving

The gearbox locks itself into a fixed ratio to survive. On CVTs this is usually overheat protection — fluid temperature spiked past safe limits, common in hill driving or long crawls.

**Urgency: high.** Once is a warning; twice is a pattern. The cooling circuit and fluid need attention before the belt pays the price.

### Metallic rattle at idle in D

Often the torque converter or a failing bearing resonating. On Punch CVTs, worn start-clutch dampers rattle at idle.

**Urgency: needs diagnosis** — rattles are cheap to inspect and expensive to ignore.

## The three-tier repair reality

| Tier | What it involves | Typical Malaysian cost |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Service | Fluid, filter, adaptation reset, software | RM 250 – 600 |
| Repair | Valve body, converter/start clutch, seals, single bearing | RM 1,500 – 3,500 |
| Overhaul | Full teardown, belt, bearings, all soft parts | RM 4,000 – 7,500 |

The pattern to notice: **each tier is roughly triple the one before it.** The whole economics of CVT ownership comes down to catching problems one tier earlier.

## What to do with a symptom

Don't start with parts — start with a diagnosis. A specialist road test plus a scan of the CVT's internal data (fluid temperature history, pressure readings, slip counters) identifies the actual fault in under an hour. It's the difference between fixing what's broken and paying to guess.
